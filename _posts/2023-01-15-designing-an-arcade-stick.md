## Designing an all-button arcade stick: ExpressBox

I've always wanted to play fighting games. They have a lot of elements that appeal to me: fun characters, a sense of both competition and camaraderie, and lots of room for mastery in gameplay. My problem came when actually trying to play them. 

For anyone unaware, most fighting games use relatively complex controls. Whereas in one game you might get a light and a heavy attack, in fighting games, you get those plus special moves, plus even more special moves. The latter two of these are usually performed by a combination of specific motions plus a button. For example, the most common motion is quarter-circle where you move the stick in the shape of 1/4 of a circle, then hit an attack button. What you get from a quarter-circle move varies from character to character and game to game, but these are often very iconic moves like Ryu's hadouken from Street Fighter. It's not uncommon for a fighting game character's total moves number in the dozens or more. This is all made even more complex by combos, strings of attacks done with precise timing to do more damage. It's not enough to just be able to do your moves -- you need to be able to do them *precisely*. 

There are two main ways to play fighting games: pad (gamepad) or stick (arcade stick). Both have their advantages and disadvantages, and players a mix of both methods even through the highest ranks of most games. For me, these two methods have one notable thing in common: they hurt. 

Fighting game matches are intense and inputs need to be performed quickly. When using a stick and especially a pad, my hands become fatigued very quickly. Now, this is not inherent problem to all people or even to me. If I practiced, I suspect I could overcome the fatigue and become fairly comfortable with either method. The problem is, I'd have to endure a lot of pain in the meantime and put in a lot of hours, and this is all just for baseline doing the movies. It's not accounting for actually learning the mental game, i.e. how to apply the raw moves in a match. 

I had pretty much resigned myself to watching fighting games until I learned about a third control method: a button box. 

## What is a button box?

To call a button box a third control method is a little bit generous. It's actually more of an offshoot of the arcade stick that simply replaces the "stick" with more buttons. You get four buttons for each cardinal direction, and holding two at the same time will give you the diagonal, so hitting Up+Right will become diagonal up and to the right. This especially works for traditional-style fighting games because these only really operate on eight directions, even when it is technically possible to have more freedom with a stick or pad. This is however not the case for other kinds of fighting games like Super Smash Bros. where fine grain directional control is extremely important. This wasn't really a problem for me, though. I like Super Smash Bros. casually, but I had no real desire to get "good" at it. Traditional fighters like Street Fighter, Guilty Gear, and BlazeBlue were what appealed to me. 

### Why does a button box change things? 

Well, quite simply, my hands don't hurt while using an all-button control scheme. They *can* start to hurt after a long time, but I immediately gain much more stamina to focus on practicing playing the game in front of me rather than learning the controller. I also think all-button controls are just more intuitive to me as someone who is very immersed in keyboards and who types a lot. 

### Why not just use a keyboard?

A very good question. Practicing on a keyboard was the first thing that showed me a button box controller could work for me. The reason I didn't want to simply stick with a keyboard is they're not that well optimized for fighting games. Keyboards focus on having tons of keys in a small space, but for fighting games, you only need a few keys or buttons. Importantly, you also need to hit the correct buttons at the correct time, so a button box controller that minimizes distractions and slightly spaces the buttons out is a huge quality-of-life improvement. Plus, it's just fun, frankly. There is a history and tradition when it comes to arcade sticks. Many people customize their controllers to reflect their personalities or interest. There is something satisfying about a specialized tool that is even further personalized just for you. 

Okay great, a button box it is. I'll just go buy one and... oh no.
